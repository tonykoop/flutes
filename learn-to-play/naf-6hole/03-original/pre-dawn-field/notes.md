# Pre-Dawn Field

The Heifer Zephyr origin tune. Composed for the A-tuned 6-hole Native
American flute as the centerpiece of the NAF starter songbook.

## The story behind the tune

A handful of friends drove north out of Minneapolis on a forecast that
the aurora would be visible. They went to Joe's family farm and stayed
up all night, lying in the field, picking out constellations. The
aurora never came. The sky stayed black and bright with stars. They
talked, dozed, talked again.

When the sun finally began to rise, the field was buried under a
shallow layer of cold fog and every breath plumed white in the air.
Standing there, breath flaring out of his nostrils into the chill, Tony
remembered a heifer bison he'd watched on a Yellowstone road trip,
hot air pouring from her nose into the cold morning. Each breath felt
crisp enough to taste.

Heifer Zephyr is named for that moment — the warm breath, the cold
field, the long night that ended with the sun and not the aurora.

This tune is the music of that night.

## Brief

- **Instrument:** Native American Flute (6-hole) (`naf-6hole`)
- **Range:** A4 – D6 (tune uses A4–A5; one octave)
- **Default key:** A
- **Scale:** A minor pentatonic (A, C, D, E, G)
- **Mood:** contemplative, slow, single-octave pentatonic;
  the all-night sky-watch into the cold-fog dawn; frost-breath
  flaring like a heifer bison's in the chill
- **Form:** AABA (8 + 8 + 8 + 8 = 32 bars)
- **Tempo:** ♩ = 60 (slow, breath-paced)
- **Meter:** 4/4
- **Build repo:** tonykoop/flutes

## How the form maps to the story

- **A1 (bars 1–8):** the long vigil. Anchored on A4, conjunct motion,
  long held tonics. The night is patient. Breath marks every 4 bars
  match the long sustained phrasing.
- **A2 (bars 9–16):** the same shape, sitting deeper. The middle of
  the night, when the conversation has gone quiet and you're just
  watching the stars rotate.
- **B (bars 17–24):** dawn rises. The melody climbs from E5 up to A5
  (the upper tonic) and stays there for a full bar — the held breath
  before the sun crests the field. One grace-note ornament at bar 21
  (`{g}a4`) is the frost-breath flaring out, a single warm puff
  against the cold.
- **A return (bars 25–32):** the melody settles back down, but
  resolves more peacefully than it began. The night is over; the cold
  is in your bones; you're still standing in the field, breath
  visible, alive.

## Composition rules followed

Per `references/flute-family.md`, NAF rules:

- Pentatonic only (A, C, D, E, G — strict, no passing tones).
- Conjunct motion throughout (largest interval is a minor third).
- Ornament budget: zero or one per phrase. Used: exactly **one**, at
  bar 21, the apex of the B section.
- Breath marks at every 4-bar phrase boundary (`!breath!`).
- Single-octave melody so beginners stay in the playable sweet spot.

## Practice tip

Hold the long notes — the dotted halves and whole notes — *as long as
your breath lasts*. The tune is built around sustains, not motion.
The high A5 (bar 18) is a full bar of held tonic; treat it as a
deliberate exhale, not a "note."

The grace note at bar 21 is the only ornament in the tune. Play it
*late* — slip into the A5 just barely before the beat, then let the
A ring. That's the frost-breath.

If you run out of air on the high A, that's OK. Take a breath right
after, and re-enter on the descent (bar 19 starts on G5). The tune
forgives.
