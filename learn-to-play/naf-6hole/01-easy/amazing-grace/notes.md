# Amazing Grace (arranged for A-NAF)

The "New Britain" melody first paired with John Newton's 1779 lyrics in
William Walker's *Southern Harmony* (1835). Both lyrics and tune are
public domain in the United States.

## What this arrangement does

The canonical version (in `catalog/public-domain/hymn/amazing-grace/`)
is in G major pentatonic and runs D4–D5 — which would put the low D
below the A-tuned NAF's playable floor of A4. This arrangement
**transposes the whole melody up one octave** so the floor is D5, well
within the NAF's range. The single B5 at each phrase apex is
substituted with **C6** (the nearest A-minor-pentatonic note above), so
the final note set is `{D5, E5, G5, A5, C6, D6}` — all playable on the
A-NAF.

Tonal centre after the substitution is **G5** rather than the original
G4; the tune now sits in the upper half of the NAF range, which is
where the flute sounds richest.

## Provenance

- **Lyrics:** John Newton, 1779 — public domain.
- **Melody ("New Britain"):** trad. American shape-note, paired with
  Newton's lyrics in Walker's 1835 *Southern Harmony* — public domain.
- **This NAF arrangement:** Tony Koop / Heifer Zephyr, 2026,
  MIT-licensed.

## Practice tip

The high register of an A-NAF needs more breath support than the lower
register — don't overblow, but commit firmly to each note. The D6 in
the second phrase is the highest note in the songbook; aim for it
gently and let it bloom rather than pushing it.

The dotted halves at every phrase end are the soul of the tune. Don't
truncate — sustain to the end of your breath, then take a fresh breath
before the next phrase. The breath itself is the rhythm.
